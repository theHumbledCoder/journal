I once spent three days building a project in Notion called "My Life." I tried to break every aspect of my existence — from meditation to personal chores — into measurable sprints. My logic was simple: if I treat my life like a sprint, I will get more done.

After setting it up, I never opened it again.

I tried the same thing with calendar blocking. I mapped out every 30-minute increment of my day, assigning specific tasks to each slot. I lasted exactly one day before the system became overwhelmingly restrictive, and I abandoned it.

These attempts were clear symptoms of a deeper problem: over-engineering. It is a common trap. We plan a comprehensive diet with perfectly tracked macros, only to eat junk food as a "reward" for making such a great plan. We design a rigid two-hour, seven-day-a-week workout regimen and never step foot in the gym.

The grander the plan, the lower the execution. Elaborate master plans are often just a defense mechanism against starting. They give us the feeling of productivity without the risk of doing things imperfectly.

## The invisible procrastination of planning

Planning can feel like making progress because it creates structure and certainty. But structure in service of avoiding the work is still avoidance. If your plan is so detailed that it never survives the first real day, it is not a system — it is a ritual of not starting.

When I was building "My Life" in Notion, I was not doing better work. I was protecting myself from the discomfort of doing work without knowing exactly how it would unfold.

## When a master plan is just a wall

A grand plan can become a wall between you and action. You convince yourself that the system is the thing that matters, when really the system is just a story you tell to avoid taking the first small step.

The more elaborate the system, the more energy it takes to maintain and the more brittle it becomes. That brittleness is the problem: one missed block, one unplanned interruption, and the whole thing collapses.

## What actually works

The antidote is not to stop planning entirely. The antidote is to make plans that are small enough to fail and simple enough to restart.

A useful system is not one you build once and worship. It is one you can re-enter without shame after you fall off. It is a structure that supports doing, not a substitute for doing.

This is the trap of the master plan: it feels responsible, but it often hides the real issue. The real issue is that we are afraid of starting imperfectly. And that fear is what keeps us stuck.
